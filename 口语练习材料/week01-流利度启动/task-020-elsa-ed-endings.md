# Task 20: ELSA Practice - -ed Endings

## 📚 PRE-STUDY [5 minutes]

### Before You Begin

**Why Focus on -ed Endings?**

Past tense -ed endings are one of the **most common pronunciation errors** for Chinese speakers—and one of the most noticeable to examiners.

**The Problem:**
Many learners pronounce ALL -ed endings as /ɪd/ (like "worked" = "work-id")

**The Reality:**
-ed has **3 different pronunciations** depending on the final sound of the verb.

### The 3 -ed Pronunciations

| Sound | When to Use | Example | IPA |
|-------|-------------|---------|-----|
| **/t/** | After voiceless sounds | worked, helped, washed | /wɜːkt/, /helpt/, /wɒʃt/ |
| **/d/** | After voiced sounds | played, called, lived | /pleɪd/, /kɔːld/, /lɪvd/ |
| **/ɪd/** | After t or d | wanted, needed, started | /wɒntɪd/, /niːdɪd/, /stɑːtɪd/ |

### Key Concepts

| Term | Meaning | Examples |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Voiceless** | No vocal cord vibration | p, k, f, s, sh, ch |
| **Voiced** | Vocal cord vibration | b, g, v, z, m, n, l, r |
| **Stop sounds** | t and d need separation | want-ed, need-ed |

### 🎤 Common -ed Ending Errors for Chinese Speakers

| Error | You Say | Should Say | IPA |
|-------|---------|-----------|-----|
| **Adding /ɪd/ to everything** | "work-id" "help-id" "play-id" | "workt" "helpt" "playd" | /wɜːkt/ /helpt/ /pleɪd/ |
| **Dropping -ed entirely** | "work" "help" (no ending) | "worked" "helped" | /wɜːkt/ /helpt/ |
| **Confusing /t/ and /d/** | "work-d" "play-t" | "workt" "playd" | /wɜːkt/ /pleɪd/ |

### The "Hum Test" for Voiced vs. Voiceless

**How to Check if a Sound is Voiced:**
1. Place your hand gently on your throat
2. Make the sound (p, b, t, d, f, v, etc.)
3. Feel vibration?

**Vibration = Voiced (/d/ ending)**
**No vibration = Voiceless (/t/ ending)**

Practice:
- /p/ → no vibration → "helped" = /helpt/
- /b/ → vibration! → "robbed" = /rɒbd/
- /k/ → no vibration → "walked" = /wɔːkt/
- /g/ → vibration! → "begged" = /begd/

## 💡 COACH'S NOTES

### 🎓 Teaching Points

**1. Why This Mistake Is So Noticeable**
> Everyone makes pronunciation errors. But -ed endings are VERY noticeable because:
> 1. They're extremely common (every past tense verb)
> 2. The error is consistent (always /ɪd/ instead of /t/ or /d/)
> 3. Native speakers NEVER make this error
>
> Fixing -ed endings = immediate improvement in how natural you sound.

**2. The "Voiced vs. Voiceless" Test**
How do you know if a sound is voiced or not?

**The Hum Test:**
- Put your hand on your throat
- Say the sound
- Feel vibration? = Voiced
- No vibration? = Voiceless

Try it:
- /p/ (no vibration) = voiceless → worked = /t/
- /b/ (vibration!) = voiced → played = /d/
- /t/ (no vibration) = voiceless BUT need separation = started = /ɪd/

**3. The Only Exception: t and d**
Words ending in t or d ALWAYS get /ɪd/
Why? Because you can't say "start-d" or "want-d" smoothly.
The /ɪ/ sound separates the two similar sounds.

### 🔧 Common Pitfalls & Fixes

| Pitfall | Problem | Coach's Fix |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Pronouncing all as /ɪd/** | Most common error | Learn the rule, practice daily |
| **Not checking the last letter** | Guessing wrong sound | Look at the final letter BEFORE adding -ed |
| **Forgetting the rule** | Reverting to old habit | Practice 5-10 minutes daily for 2 weeks |
| **Only practicing isolated words** | Doesn't transfer to speech | Always practice sentences too |
| **Rushing through** | No muscle memory | Slow down, deliberate practice |
| **Not reviewing tomorrow** | Forgetting quickly | Space repetition: practice again tomorrow |

### 🎤 Self-Monitoring: How to Hear Your -ed Errors

**1. The "Exaggerate First" Method**
- Say "work-TID" very loudly, exaggerating the T
- Say "play-DID" very loudly, exaggerating the D
- THEN gradually reduce to "workt" and "playd"
- Your muscle memory will hold the correct sound

**2. Recording Check**
- Record yourself saying: "I worked, played, wanted, stopped, called"
- Listen specifically for the ending sounds
- Compare with a native speaker recording
- Can you hear the difference between /t/, /d/, and /ɪd/?

**3. The "Quick Test" for Any Word**
When you see a past tense verb:
1. What's the LAST letter of the base verb?
2. Is it voiced or voiceless? (use hum test)
3. Decision:
   - Voiceless (p, k, f, s, sh, ch) → /t/
   - Voiced (b, g, v, z, m, n, l, r) → /d/
   - t or d → /ɪd/

**Practice this decision process for 2 weeks—it becomes automatic!**

### 🎯 Pro Tips

1. **Keep a list** - write down common -ed words you use
2. **Use the hum test** - check voiced vs. voiceless with your hand
3. **Practice in sentences** - context helps memory
4. **Record yourself** - compare your pronunciation to ELSA's model
5. **5 words a day** - consistent practice beats cramming

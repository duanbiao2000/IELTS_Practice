# Task 80: Recording – Test Your Universal Examples

## 📚 PRE-STUDY [5 minutes]

### Before You Begin
**Why record your universal examples?**

Theory is not practice. You may think your stories are versatile, but:
- **Reality check** – can you actually adapt them under pressure?
- **Fluency verification** – does the story flow naturally in different contexts?
- **Gap identification** – where does the adaptation break down?
- **Confidence building** – successful adaptation = exam insurance

## 📝 MODEL ANSWER & REFERENCE

### Sample Universal Example in Action

**Core Story:** Learning to cook during lockdown

**Adaptation 1 – Topic: "Describe a skill you learned"**
"One skill I learned during the pandemic was cooking. Like many people, I found myself at home with a lot of extra time, so I decided to teach myself how to cook proper meals..."

**Adaptation 2 – Topic: "Describe a change in your life"**
"A significant change in my life occurred during the COVID-19 lockdown. Suddenly, I had to prepare all my own meals, which forced me to learn cooking from scratch..."

**Adaptation 3 – Topic: "Describe something you did to stay healthy"**
"During the lockdown, I started cooking my own meals as a way to stay healthy. I realized that restaurant food often has too much oil and salt, so learning to cook gave me control..."

### What Makes Adaptation Work

| Element | Strong Adaptation | Weak Adaptation |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Connection phrase** | "This relates to [topic] because..." | Just launches into story |
| **Emphasis shift** | Highlights different aspects each time | Same emphasis regardless of topic |
| **Opening line** | Tailored to specific topic | Generic opening |
| **Vocabulary** | Uses topic-relevant words | Same words every time |

### Common Adaptation Challenges

| Challenge | Example | Fix |
|-----------|---------|-----|
| **Forced connection** | Using a cooking story for "Describe a building" | Choose a more relevant universal example |
| **Repetitive phrasing** | Starting every answer with "So..." | Vary your openings: "Well...", "Actually...", "To be honest..." |
| **Losing the point** | Story goes on, forgets to address topic | Keep bringing it back to the topic question |
| **Obvious template** | Examiner can tell you're reciting | Make it sound spontaneous each time |

## 💡 COACH'S NOTES

### 🎓 Teaching Points

**1. The Adaptation Formula**
> Successful adaptation = Connection + Emphasis + Vocabulary
> - **Connection**: Explicitly link your story to the topic
> - **Emphasis**: Highlight different aspects based on the angle
> - **Vocabulary**: Use words from the topic domain

**2. Why Natural Matters More Than Clever**
A simple story that fits naturally scores better than a clever adaptation that feels forced. Examiners value authenticity over originality.

**3. The "Fit Test"**
After speaking, ask yourself:
- Did I answer the question directly?
- Did my story support my answer?
- Would an examiner understand the connection?
- If any answer is "no," the adaptation didn't work.

### 🔧 Common Pitfalls & Fixes

| Pitfall | Coach's Fix |
|---------|-------------|
| **Universal examples aren't universal** | Some stories just don't work for multiple topics. That's okay – accept it and develop others |
| **Forgetting the adaptation** | You planned the angle but got nervous and reverted to default. Practice more! |
| **Obvious recitation** | You sound like you're reading from a script. Vary your pacing, add natural pauses |
| **Time mismanagement** | Your story is too long for the topic. Practice condensing versions |

### 🎯 Pro Tips

1. **Create "flex points"** in your stories – moments where you can expand or contract
2. **Practice transition phrases** – "What this taught me was...", "The reason I mention this is..."
3. **Note your best adaptations** – these are your go-to strategies for the exam
4. **Compare your recordings** – which versions sounded most natural? Why?

# Task 26: Relative Clause Patterns

## 📚 PRE-STUDY [10 minutes]

### Before You Begin
**Why are relative clauses important for IELTS?**

Relative clauses are **complexity builders**. They allow you to:
- Add detailed information without starting new sentences
- Demonstrate grammatical range (Band 7+ requirement)
- Sound more sophisticated and natural
- Connect ideas smoothly

### Key Concepts

**Relative Clause Formula:**
```
[Thing/Person] + which/that/who + [verb phrase]...
```

| Relative Word | Usage | Example |
|---------------|-------|---------|
| **which** | Things, ideas, places | The book which changed my life... |
| **that** | Things (more common in speaking) | The movie that I watched... |
| **who** | People | The teacher who inspired me... |
| **where** | Places | The city where I was born... |
| **when** | Time | The year when I graduated... |

### Sentence Patterns

**Pattern 1: Describing things**
- "The technology which has transformed my life is..."
- "One tradition that I really enjoy is..."

**Pattern 2: Describing people**
- "My friend who lives abroad..."
- "The teacher who taught me English..."

**Pattern 3: Adding context**
- "My hometown, which is located in the north..."
- "The restaurant where we met..."

## 🎯 GUIDED PRACTICE

### Task Overview
Practice relative clause patterns by creating 5 sentences and speaking them aloud.

### Step-by-Step Instructions

#### STEP 1: Study the Examples [3 min]

**Example 1 (Simple → Complex):**
- Simple: I like smartphones. They help me communicate.
- Complex: "I love smartphones, **which have become essential** for daily communication."

**Example 2 (Simple → Complex):**
- Simple: I have a friend. He studies abroad.
- Complex: "I have a friend **who is currently studying** in the UK."

**Example 3 (Simple → Complex):**
- Simple: I visited a city. It has beautiful beaches.
- Complex: "I visited a city **which is famous for its** stunning beaches."

## 💡 COACH'S NOTES

### 🎓 Teaching Points

**1. Why "That" vs. "Which"?**
- In **speaking**, "that" is more common and natural
- "Which" sounds slightly more formal
- For IELTS, both are acceptable—use what feels natural

**2. Position Matters**
- The relative clause must **immediately follow** the noun it describes
- ✅ "The book **which I read** yesterday was interesting."
- ❌ "The book was interesting **which I read** yesterday."

**3. Don't Overuse**
- Not every sentence needs a relative clause
- Mix simple and complex sentences
- Aim for 2-3 relative clauses per 1-minute response

### 🔧 Common Pitfalls & Fixes

| Pitfall | Example | Coach's Fix |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Wrong relative word** | "The man **which** helped me" | "The man **who** helped me" (people = who) |
| **Missing verb** | "The book **which** interesting" | "The book **which is** interesting" |
| **Dangling clause** | "I like cars. **Which are fast**." | "I like cars, **which are fast**." (combine!) |
| **Repetition** | "My friend **who** is nice **who** helps me" | "My friend **who** is nice **and** helps me" |

### 🎯 Pro Tips

1. **Start with "that"**—it's the safest and most common
2. **Practice the rhythm**: noun → slight pause → relative clause → continue
3. **Memorize 3-4 go-to patterns** you can use in any topic
4. **Record yourself**—relative clauses can sound rushed if spoken too quickly

# Task 67: ELSA - Linking Rules (Consonant + Vowel)

## 📚 PRE-STUDY [4 minutes]

### Before You Begin
**Why linking matters for fluency?**

Native speakers don't say words separately—they LINK them. Understanding linking:
- **Makes your speech sound more natural**
- **Improves your rhythm** (crucial for fluency scores)
- **Helps you understand native speech** (you'll hear what's actually being said)
- **Reduces the "choppy" non-native sound**

### What Is Linking?

Linking = connecting the end of one word to the start of the next.

**Example:**
- Separate words: "pick_it_up" (3 distinct words)
- Linked speech: "pi-ki-tup" (sounds like 1 word)

### The Main Linking Rule: Consonant + Vowel

When a word ending in a **consonant** is followed by a word starting with a **vowel**, link them:

```
Word 1 (ends in consonant) + Word 2 (starts with vowel)
                    ↓
              LINK THEM TOGETHER
```

**Examples with IPA:**
- "pick it up" → /pɪkɪtʌp/ (not /pɪk ɪt ʌp/)
- "red apple" → /rædæpl/ (not /ræd æpl/)
- "check it" → /tʃɛkɪt/ (not /tʃɛk ɪt/)

### Linking Error Patterns for Chinese Speakers

| Error Type | Unlinked (Choppy) | Linked (Natural) | IPA Comparison |
|------------|-------------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **No linking** | pick / it / up | pick-it-up | /pɪk ɪt ʌp/ → /pɪkɪtʌp/ |
| **Too much break** | red / apple | red_apple | /rɛd æpl/ → /rædæpl/ |
| **Adding glottal stops** | check ↑ it | check_it | /tʃɛk ʔ ɪt/ → /tʃɛkɪt/ |
| **Dropping final consonant** | pi_kit_up | pick_it_up | /pɪkɪtʌp/ (keep the /k/!) |

### Physical Awareness for Linking

**How linking FEELS physically:**

1. **Continuous airflow**: Don't stop air between words
2. **No pause in jaw**: Keep jaw moving smoothly
3. **Consonant "jump"**: Final consonant flows into next vowel
4. **No "break" feeling**: Speech feels like ONE continuous stream

**Physical cue:**
- Say "pick...it...up" (unlinked) → feel the stops
- Say "pick-it-up" (linked) → feel the FLOW
- Linking feels like a smooth river, not choppy waves

### Step-by-Step Instructions

#### STEP 1: Understand the Rule [2 min]

**Consonant + Vowel Linking:**
When Word A ends in a consonant and Word B starts with a vowel, the consonant "jumps" to the start of Word B.

**Visual:**
```
pick     it      up
 ↓        ↓        ↓
[p]   +  [i]  +  [t]
        ↓
    [p] jumps to [i]
    [i] jumps to [t]
Result: pi-ki-tup
```

### Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|---------|----------------|------------------|
| **Not linking at all** | Sounds choppy, unnatural | Actively connect consonant to vowel |
| **Over-linking** | Not every connection links | Only C+V, not every word pair |
| **Linking vowels incorrectly** | V+V uses different rules | V+V adds /j/ or /w/ sounds |
| **Linking across pauses** | Don't link across breath groups | Only link within phrases |
| **Forgetting in sentences** | Practice carries over | Apply in real speech |

## 💡 COACH'S NOTES

### 🎓 Teaching Points

**1. Linking Creates Flow**
The biggest difference between "choppy" non-native speech and smooth native speech is linking. Native speakers don't say word-by-word—they flow from sound to sound, regardless of word boundaries.

**2. The Physical Sensation**
When you link correctly, you can FEEL it:
- Your tongue doesn't stop between words
- The speech feels continuous
- There are no "breaks" in the sound stream

**3. ELSA Can Hear Linking**
ELSA scores often improve when you link correctly. The app hears the flow as more native-like. Practice linking and watch your scores go up!

### 🔧 Common Pitfalls & Fixes

| Pitfall | Coach's Fix |
|---------|-------------|
| **Feeling self-conscious about linking** | Linking is CORRECT English, not slang. It's how native speakers speak |
| **Over-thinking the rule** | Practice until it's automatic. Linking should feel natural, not calculated |
| **Only practicing isolated phrases** | Apply linking in real sentences and speech |
| **Linking across breath groups** | Pause for breath, then start fresh. Don't link across pauses |
| **Giving up when scores don't improve** | Linking takes practice. Keep at it |

### 🎯 Pro Tips

1. **Start with common phrases**: "pick it up," "check it," "look at"
2. **Use a mirror**: Watch your tongue NOT stopping between words
3. **Record and compare**: Link your version vs. non-linked version
4. **Practice daily**: Linking needs repetition to become automatic

### 🎤 Linking Self-Monitoring Guide

**How to Check Your Own Linking:**

**Step 1: The "Pause Test"**
- Record yourself saying: "pick it up"
- Listen for: Do you hear pauses between words?
- Linked: /pɪkɪtʌp/ (no pauses)
- Unlinked: /pɪk... ɪt... ʌp/ (pauses heard)

**Step 2: The Physical Check**
- Say "pick it up" with linking
- Feel: Tongue should NOT stop between words
- The /k/ sound should flow directly into /ɪ/
- The /t/ should flow directly into /ʌ/
- No "break" in the speech stream

**Step 3: Visual Marking Practice**
Write phrases and mark the links with arrows:

```
pick↑it↑up
check↑it↑out
look↑at↑it
turn↑it↑on
wake↑up
```

**Step 4: Progress Recording**
Week 1: Record 5 phrases, count linking errors
Week 2: Practice daily, re-record same phrases
Week 3: Compare - fewer pauses = better linking

**Common Linking Mistakes to Avoid:**

| Mistake | Example | Why It's Wrong | Correct Version |
|---------|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Adding glottal stop** | pick↑↑it | Extra break between words | pickit (smooth) |
| **Dropping final consonant** | pi_kit_up | Loses the /k/ sound | pick_it_up (keep all sounds) |
| **Linking EVERYTHING** | pick↑and↑choose | Only link C+V, not C+C | pick and choose (pause before and) |
| **Linking across breath** | turn↑it↑on↑and↑go | Breaks natural phrasing | turniton [breath] andgo |

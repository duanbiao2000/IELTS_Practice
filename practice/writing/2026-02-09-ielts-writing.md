## Task 1: Academic Writing (Line Graph)

### Topic: Internet Usage in Three Countries (2000-2020)

**Task Type:** Line Graph
**Time:** 20 minutes
**Word Count:** ~180 words

---

### The Graph

The line graph illustrates the percentage of population using the internet in the United States, Japan, and South Korea over a 20-year period from 2000 to 2020.

---

### Model Essay (178 words)

The line graph provides a clear comparison of internet adoption rates among three developed nations—the United States, Japan, and South Korea—spanning from 2000 to 2020. Overall, all three countries experienced a significant upward trend in internet usage, with South Korea ultimately achieving the highest percentage.

In 2000, the United States led with approximately 45% of its population accessing the internet, while Japan and South Korea followed at 30% and 20% respectively. Over the next decade, all three nations demonstrated steady growth. By 2010, the US had reached 80%, a remarkable increase of 35 percentage points. Japan's growth was equally impressive, climbing from 30% to 75%, whereas South Korea showed the most dramatic surge, tripling from 20% to 60%.

The decade from 2010 to 2020 saw further improvements, though at a more gradual pace. The US maintained its leading position, peaking at 92% in 2020. Japan continued its steady ascent, reaching 89%, while South Korea closed the gap substantially, achieving 95%—the highest among the three countries. Notably, the gap between the nations narrowed significantly in the final years, reflecting the near-universal internet access that characterizes developed economies today.

## Task 2: Essay Writing (Agree/Disagree)

### Topic: Remote Work and Urban Depopulation

**Prompt:** Some people believe that widespread adoption of remote work will lead to the depopulation of major cities. To what extent do you agree or disagree?

**Task Type:** Agree/Disagree
**Time:** 40 minutes
**Word Count:** 312 words

---

### Model Essay

The shift towards remote work, accelerated by the global pandemic, has sparked intense debate about the future of urban centers. While some argue that this trend will inevitably lead to city depopulation, I largely disagree with this view. I believe that while cities may experience demographic shifts, their fundamental economic and cultural importance will ensure their continued vitality.

Firstly, major cities possess irreplaceable advantages that remote work cannot replicate. These urban centers serve as hubs for innovation, boasting concentrations of talent, venture capital, and specialized industries. Silicon Valley, for instance, thrives not merely because companies allow remote work, but because of the spontaneous collaborations, networking opportunities, and knowledge spillovers that occur when ambitious professionals congregate physically. The symbiotic relationship between universities, research institutions, and corporations creates an ecosystem that virtual work environments cannot fully substitute.

Furthermore, cities offer lifestyle amenities and cultural experiences that remote locations struggle to provide. Young professionals, in particular, value the access to diverse dining scenes, entertainment venues, museums, and social networks that urban environments facilitate. The human desire for community and face-to-face interaction is deeply ingrained; video calls and virtual meetings, while convenient, cannot completely replace the richness of in-person relationships and the serendipitous encounters that shape personal and professional growth.

Admittedly, certain demographic groups may opt for suburban or rural living, attracted by lower housing costs and larger living spaces. This could lead to a redistribution of population rather than outright depopulation. However, this shift is likely to be balanced by the influx of workers seeking the unique career opportunities and social dynamism that only major cities can offer.

In conclusion, while remote work will undoubtedly reshape how people live and work, it will not render cities obsolete. Their role as engines of innovation, cultural beacons, and social magnets ensures that urban centers will adapt and thrive in this new era of work. The future is not a choice between cities and remote work, but rather a hybrid model where both coexist and complement each other.

## 💡 Writing Tips for Band 7.5+

### Task 1 (Line Graph)

1. **Overview Positioning:** Place overview in a separate paragraph after introduction. State the main trends clearly without specific data.

2. **Data Accuracy:** Report numbers precisely. Use "approximately," "roughly," "nearly" when estimates are needed.

3. **Time Markers:** Use varied time expressions (In 2000, Over the decade, By 2010, During the final period).

4. **Comparison Language:** Use comparative and superlative forms appropriately (higher than, the highest, exceeded, surpassed).

5. **Trend Verbs:** Increase: rose, climbed, increased, surged, jumped. Decrease: fell, declined, dropped, decreased, plunged.

6. **Avoid:** Describing every single data point. Select key features and trends.

---

### Task 2 (Agree/Disagree)

1. **Clear Position:** State your opinion clearly in introduction. Use nuanced language ("I largely agree") rather than absolutes.

2. **Balanced Reasoning:** Acknowledge counter-arguments in separate paragraph. This shows critical thinking.

3. **Paragraph Structure:** Topic sentence → Explanation → Example → Link to next point. Each paragraph should have one clear main idea.

4. **Sentence Variety:** Mix simple, compound, and complex sentences. Don't overuse one structure.

5. **Specific Examples:** Use real-world examples (Silicon Valley) to support arguments. Abstract arguments are less convincing.

6. **Conclusion:** Restate thesis without repetition. No new information. Summarize main points briefly.

7. **Avoid:** Overgeneralizations ("Everyone thinks..."), first-person in every sentence, emotional language without evidence.

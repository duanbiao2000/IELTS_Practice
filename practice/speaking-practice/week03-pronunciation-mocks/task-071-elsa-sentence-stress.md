# Task 71: ELSA - Sentence Stress Training

## 📚 PRE-STUDY [4 minutes]

### Before You Begin
**Why sentence stress matters?**

English stress is like music—some beats are loud (stressed), others are quiet (unstressed). Native speakers automatically stress only 1-2 keywords per sentence. When you stress too many words:
- **You sound robotic** (like a robot, not a human)
- **Your rhythm is wrong** (English has specific stress patterns)
- **It's hard to follow** (listeners can't identify the main point)
- **Your fluency score drops** (stress = 25% of pronunciation score)

### The Golden Rule of English Stress

> **1-2 keywords per sentence maximum.**

Everything else is unstressed (weak forms, schwa sounds).

### How English Stress Works

```
Stressed word:  LOUDER + LONGER + HIGHER PITCH
Unstressed:     quieter + shorter + lower pitch
```

**Example with IPA:**
"I **LOVE** reading **BOOKS**." → /aɪ **ˈlʌv** ˈriːdɪŋ **ˈbʊks**/ (2 stressed words)
NOT: "I **love** **reading** **books**." (3 stressed = too many!)

### Chinese Speaker Stress Error Patterns

| Error Type | Unstressed Word Should Be | Common Error | IPA Correction |
|------------|---------------------------|--------------|----------------|
| **Stressing articles** | the (schwa /ðə/) | /ðiː/ (strong) | /ðə/ in unstressed |
| **Stressing prepositions** | of, for, to (weak) | /ɒv/, /fɔːr/, /tuː/ (strong) | /əv/, /fər/, /tə/ (weak) |
| **Stressing pronouns** | I, you, he, she (weak) | /aɪ/, /juː/, /hiː/ (strong) | /ə/, /jə/, /hi/ (when unstressed) |
| **Stressing auxiliary verbs** | is, are, have (weak) | /ɪz/, /ɑːr/, /hæv/ (strong) | /əz/, /ər/, /həv/ (weak) |
| **Too many stresses** | 1-2 per sentence | 5-6 stressed words | Reduce to 1-2 KEYWORDS only |

### Physical Awareness of Stress

**How STRESSED feels physically:**
- LOUD: More air from lungs
- LONG: Hold the vowel longer
- HIGH: Pitch goes up slightly
- CLEAR: Full pronunciation of vowel

**How UNSTRESSED feels physically:**
- Quiet: Less air
- Short: Quick pronunciation
- Low: Pitch goes down
- REDUCED: Vowel often becomes schwa /ə/

**The Schwa (/ə/) - Most Common Sound in English:**
- Unstressed vowels → schwa
- "a" → /ə/ (about /əˈbaʊt/)
- "the" → /ðə/ (the book /ðə bʊk/)
- "for" → /fər/ (for you /fər ju/)
- "to" → /tə/ (to go /tə ɡoʊ/)

## 📝 MODEL ANSWER & REFERENCE

### Sentence Stress Reference

**General Rule:** Stress the words that carry the main meaning.

| Sentence Type | What to Stress | Examples |
|---------------|----------------|----------|
| **Simple statements** | Main verb + object | "**LOVE** **BOOKS**" |
| **Descriptions** | Main adjectives/nouns | "**BEAUTIFUL** **WEATHER**" |
| **Opinions** | Key opinion words | "**THINK** **IMPORTANT**" |
| **Questions** | Question words | "**WHERE** **GOING**?" |
| **Negatives** | Negative word | "**NOT** **LIKE**" |

### Common Stress Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It's Wrong | Correct Version |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Stressing function words** | "THE book is ON the table" | "The **BOOK** is on the **TABLE**" |
| **Stressing every word equally** | Sounds robotic | Stress only 1-2 keywords |
| **Stressing too many words** | Hard to follow, unnatural | Minimal stress is better |
| **Stressing the wrong words** | Confuses meaning | Stress content words, not function words |
| **No stress at all** | Flat, monotonous | 1-2 stressed words give rhythm |

### Stress Position Examples

**Content Words (stressed):**
- Nouns: **book**, **teacher**, **London**
- Main verbs: **love**, **think**, **study**
- Adjectives: **happy**, **beautiful**, **difficult**
- Adverbs: **quickly**, **very**, **always**

**Function Words (unstressed):**
- Articles: **a**, **an**, **the**
- Prepositions: **in**, **on**, **at**, **of**
- Pronouns: **I**, **you**, **he**, **she**
- Auxiliary verbs: **is**, **are**, **have**, **will**
- Conjunctions: **and**, **but**, **or**

### How to Practice Sentence Stress

**Step 1:** Identify the 1-2 most important words
**Step 2:** Make those words LOUDER + LONGER + HIGHER
**Step 3:** Make all other words quieter + shorter + lower
**Step 4:** Practice in ELSA for feedback
**Step 5:** Record yourself and compare to native speakers

## 💡 COACH'S NOTES

### 🎓 Teaching Points

**1. English Is Stress-Timed**
Some languages are syllable-timed (every syllable equal). English is stress-timed (stressed syllables equal, unstressed compressed). This is why sentence stress matters—it's the foundation of English rhythm.

**2. The "Rubber Band" Analogy**
Think of unstressed syllables as a compressed rubber band. They're short and quick. Stressed syllables are the stretched rubber band—long, loud, and prominent.

**3. Fewer Stresses = More Natural**
Beginners stress too many words. Advanced speakers stress fewer. Counter-intuitively, reducing stress makes you sound MORE native, not less.

### 🔧 Common Pitfalls & Fixes

| Pitfall | Coach's Fix |
|---------|-------------|
| **Stressing every word equally** | Identify the 1-2 KEYWORDS only. Stress those, nothing else |
| **Stressing function words** | Articles, prepositions, pronouns = unstressed. Always. |
| **Not stressing enough** | You DO need stress. Just 1-2 words, not zero. |
| **Stressing the wrong words** | Stress content words (nouns, verbs, adjectives). Not function words. |
| **Inconsistent stress** | The same word should be stressed the same way each time |

### 🎯 Pro Tips

1. **The "Beat" Test**: Tap a beat for each STRESSED syllable only. You should tap 1-2 times per sentence.
2. **Underline keywords**: When practicing, underline the 1-2 words to stress. It trains your eye and ear.
3. **Record and compare**: Record a native speaker, mark where they stress, then copy exactly.
4. **ELSA is your friend**: The app gives immediate feedback on stress. Use it!

### 🎤 Sentence Stress Self-Monitoring Guide

**How to Check Your Own Sentence Stress:**

**Step 1: The Beat Test**
- Read a sentence aloud
- Tap your finger for each STRESSED word only
- Result: Should tap 1-2 times per sentence max
- Example: "I LOVE reading BOOKS" → tap on LOVE, tap on BOOKS (2 taps)

**Step 2: The Volume Test**
- Record yourself saying a sentence
- Listen: Can you hear the VOLUME difference?
- Stressed words = LOUD
- Unstressed words = quiet
- If no volume difference, you're not stressing enough

**Step 3: The Duration Test**
- Say: "I LOVE reading books"
- Time: "LOVE" and "BOOKS" should take LONGER to say
- "I", "reading" should be QUICK
- Stressed = longer vowels, unstressed = reduced vowels

**Step 4: Visual Marking Practice**
Mark stress before speaking:

```
I went to the shop and bought a book.
↓
I **WENT** to the **SHOP** and **BOUGHT** a **BOOK**.
```

Problem: 5 stresses! Too many.
Better: Reduce to 1-2 main keywords only.

**Common Stress Errors - Self-Correction:**

| Error | Example | Why It's Wrong | Correction |
|-------|---------|----------------|------------|
| **Stressing "the"** | "**THE** book" | "The" is never stressed (unless emphatic) | "the **BOOK**" |
| **Stressing all content words** | "**WENT** to **SHOP** and **BOUGHT** **BOOK**" | Too many = robotic | Choose 1-2 MAIN ideas only |
| **Not stressing enough** | "I went to the shop" (flat) | Unclear what's important | "I **WENT** to the **SHOP**" |
| **Inconsistent stress** | "THE book" (today) vs "the BOOK" (tomorrow) | Confusing for listeners | Be consistent with stress patterns |

**Weekly Stress Practice Plan:**

- **Day 1-2**: Learn to identify keywords (content words)
- **Day 3-4**: Practice with 1-word stress sentences
- **Day 5-7**: Practice with 2-word stress sentences
- **Day 8-14**: Apply in real speech, recording and checking

**IELTS Impact:**

| IELTS Score | Stress Quality | Description |
|-------------|----------------|-------------|
| Band 5+ | Some stress attempts | Inconsistent but trying |
| Band 6+ | Generally correct stress | Mostly 1-2 keywords, some errors |
| Band 7+ | Consistent natural stress | 1-2 keywords, rhythm sounds native-like |
| Band 8+ | Sophisticated stress use | Stress for emphasis and contrast |

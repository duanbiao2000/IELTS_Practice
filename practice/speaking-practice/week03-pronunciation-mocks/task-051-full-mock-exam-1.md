# Task 51: Full Mock Exam ①

## 📚 PRE-STUDY [5 minutes]

### Before You Begin
**Why do full mock exams matter?**

A full mock exam is your reality check. It's not enough to practice individual parts—you need to experience the **full 11-minute test** as one continuous experience. This builds:
- Stamina for sustained speaking
- Mental transitions between parts
- Time management awareness
- Real exam pressure simulation

### 🎤 Pronunciation Focus for Mock Exams

**During this mock exam, pay special attention to:**

| Pronunciation Element | What to Listen For | Self-Monitor During Exam |
| ---------------------- | ------------------- | ------------------------ |
| **Problem sounds** | /θ/, /ð/, /æ/, /r/ vs /l/ | Are you aware when these occur? |
| **Word endings** | -ed, -s, -es | Are you dropping final sounds? |
| **Weak forms** | a, an, the, and, of, for | Are you using /ə/ (schwa) in unstressed positions? |
| **Sentence stress** | 1-2 keywords per sentence | Are you stressing too many words? |
| **Intonation** | Pitch variation | Is your speech flat or expressive? |

**Chinese Speaker Common Errors to Watch:**
- "think" → /sɪŋk/ (should be /θɪŋk/)
- "very" → /wɛri/ (should be /vɛri/)
- "man" → /mɛn/ (should be /mæn/)
- "world" → /wɜːld/ (should be /wɜːrld/ with clear /l/)
- Dropping -ed endings: "asked" → /æsk/ (should be /æskt/)

### IELTS Speaking Test Structure

| Part | Duration | What You'll Do | Time Awareness |
|------|----------|----------------|----------------|
| **Part 1** | 4-5 minutes | Familiar topics: work, home, hobbies, preferences | Quick responses, 2-3 sentences per question |
| **Part 2** | 3-4 minutes (1 min prep + 2 min speak) | Long turn with topic card | Must speak for full 2 minutes |
| **Part 3** | 4-5 minutes | Discussion related to Part 2 topic | Abstract ideas, extended answers |

### Mock Exam Rules
1. **NO PAUSES** between parts—simulate real exam flow
2. **TIMING STRICTLY**—use timer or stopwatch
3. **RECORD EVERYTHING**—full audio recording
4. **NO NOTES** during Part 1 and Part 3 (Part 2: 1 minute prep with pencil/paper only)

## 🎯 GUIDED PRACTICE

### Task Overview
Complete your first full mock exam with realistic timing and continuous recording.

### Step-by-Step Instructions

#### STEP 1: Set Up Your Testing Space [2 min]
- Quiet room with no distractions
- Phone/tablet for recording (test audio quality first)
- Timer visible (phone countdown or physical timer)
- Pen and paper ready (for Part 2 notes only)

#### STEP 2: Choose Your Test Prompts

**Part 1 Questions (select ONE set):**

*Set A: Home & Accommodation*
1. Do you live in a house or an apartment?
2. Which is your favourite room?
3. Would you like to move to a different home in the future?

*Set B: Work or Studies*
1. Do you work or are you a student?
2. Why did you choose this field?
3. Is there anything you'd like to change about your work/studies?

**Part 2 Topic Card:**

```
Describe a book you have read recently.
You should say:
- What the book was
- When you read it
- What it was about
- And explain whether you enjoyed it or not.
```

**Part 3 Discussion Questions:**
1. Do you think reading habits are changing in your country?
2. What types of books are most popular among young people?
3. How can parents encourage children to read more?
4. Do you think e-books will replace physical books?

#### STEP 3: Run the Mock Exam [11-14 min]

**Timeline:**
```
START RECORDING NOW

[0:00-4:00]   Part 1: Answer examiner questions
[4:00-5:00]   Part 2: 1 minute preparation (make notes)
[5:00-7:00]   Part 2: 2 minutes speaking (don't stop!)
[7:00-11:00]  Part 3: Discussion questions

STOP RECORDING
```

**During the exam:**
- Speak clearly and at natural volume
- If you stumble, keep going—don't restart
- In Part 2, aim for the full 2 minutes
- In Part 3, give extended answers (3+ sentences)

#### STEP 4: Save Your Recording [1 min]
- Label your file: `mock-001-[date].m4a` or `.mp3`
- Save in a dedicated folder for mock exams
- Do NOT listen to it yet—that's Task 52!

## 📝 MODEL ANSWER & REFERENCE

### Sample Part 1 Response (Set B - Work/Studies)

**Q: Do you work or are you a student?**

> "I'm currently a university student, in my third year studying business administration. I'm focusing on marketing this semester, which I find really interesting because it combines psychology with practical business skills."

### Sample Part 2 Response (Book Topic - 2-minute structure)

> "I'd like to talk about a book I read recently called 'Atomic Habits' by James Clear. I finished reading it about two months ago, during my summer break.
>
> The book is essentially about how small changes in our daily routines can lead to remarkable results over time. Clear explains that we often focus on setting big goals, but what really matters are the systems we build and the tiny habits we repeat every single day.
>
> What I loved most about this book was how practical it was. Each chapter had specific strategies I could apply immediately. For example, I started using 'habit stacking'—attaching a new habit to an existing one. Now, every time I make my morning coffee, I spend two minutes reviewing my goals for the day.
>
> I definitely enjoyed reading it. In fact, it's probably one of the most impactful non-fiction books I've ever read because it actually changed my behavior. I've already recommended it to several friends, and I'm planning to read it again soon to reinforce the concepts."

### Sample Part 3 Response (Extended Answer)

**Q: Do you think reading habits are changing in your country?**

> "Yes, absolutely. I've noticed a significant shift in reading habits in my country over the past decade or so. On one hand, digital reading has exploded—more people are reading articles, blogs, and social media content on their phones. This has made reading more accessible and convenient, especially for younger generations who are glued to their screens.
>
> However, there's also been concerning decline in deep reading of full-length books. Many people now struggle to focus on longer texts because they're so accustomed to short, bite-sized content. I think this is partly due to our fast-paced lifestyle and the constant distractions from notifications and social media.
>
> That said, there's also a growing movement of people consciously making time for reading—book clubs are becoming popular, and I see more people reading in cafes or on public transport. So while the medium is definitely shifting toward digital, I believe the desire for substantive reading content remains strong."

### What Makes These Responses Effective

| Element | Why It Works | Example |
|---------|--------------|---------|
| **Specific details** | Shows vocabulary range | "third year," "business administration" |
| **Personal connection** | Makes answer authentic | "I find really interesting" |
| **Clear structure** | Easy to follow | "On one hand... However... That said..." |
| **Extended reasoning** | Shows critical thinking | Part 3 answers explore multiple perspectives |
| **Natural linking** | Improves fluency score | "In fact," "That said," "So while..." |

## 💡 COACH'S NOTES

### 🎓 Teaching Points

**1. Why This Mock Exam Matters**
> This is your FIRST full simulation. Don't expect perfection. The goal is to expose weaknesses so you can target them in Week 3. A poor mock exam is MORE valuable than a good one because it shows you exactly what to work on.

**2. The Mental Transition Between Parts**
- Part 1 → Part 2: Switch from quick responses to extended monologue
- Part 2 → Part 3: Switch from personal experience to abstract discussion
- Practice this mental shift—it's crucial for exam day

**3. Timing Awareness**
- In Part 2, most students speak for only 60-90 seconds. You need to PUSH for 2 minutes
- In Part 3, aim for 3-4 sentences minimum per question

### 🎤 Pronunciation Self-Correction Guide

**How to Monitor and Correct Your Pronunciation During the Exam:**

**Step 1: Pre-Exam Pronunciation Checklist**
- Focus on your TOP 3 problem sounds (e.g., /θ/, /æ/, /r/ vs /l/)
- Say a quick practice word for each: "think," "cat," "red"
- Feel the mouth position for each

**Step 2: During the Exam - Awareness Cues**

| Pronunciation Element | Self-Correction Cue |
|-----------------------|-------------------|
| **th sounds (/θ/, /ð/)** | Before speaking words with "th", briefly touch tongue to teeth |
| **/æ/ sound (cat, man)** | Open mouth WIDE like at the dentist |
| **/r/ vs /l/ confusion** | For /r/, pull tongue back. For /l/, tap tongue behind teeth. |
| **Weak forms** | Consciously relax on "the", "and", "of" → say /ðə/, /ən/, /əv/ |
| **Sentence stress** | Identify 1-2 keywords before speaking, emphasize those only |

**Step 3: Post-Recording Analysis (Task 52)**

After this mock exam, listen specifically for:
1. **Problem sounds**: Count how many times you mispronounced your target sounds
2. **Word endings**: Did you drop -ed, -s, or other final consonants?
3. **Weak forms**: Are you saying "the" as /ðiː/ everywhere? (should be /ðə/ mostly)
4. **Stress patterns**: Are you stressing too many words per sentence?

### Most Common Chinese Speaker Pronunciation Errors

| Error Type | You Say | Correct IPA | Physical Correction |
|------------|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| **th → s** | "sink" | /θɪŋk/ | Tongue BETWEEN teeth, blow air through |
| **th → z** | "zis" | /ðɪs/ | Tongue between teeth + VOICE (vibrate cords) |
| **æ → e** | "men" (for man) | /mæn/ | Open mouth WIDE, tongue low |
| **v → w** | "wery" | /vɛri/ | Top teeth on bottom lip, vibrate voice |
| **r → l** | "lice" (for rice) | /raɪs/ | Curl tongue BACK, don't touch roof of mouth |
| **Dropped -ed** | "ask" (for asked) | /æskt/ | Add /t/ sound clearly at end |
| **Dropped -s** | "book" (for books) | /bʊks/ | Add /s/ sound clearly at end |
| **Strong "the"** | /ðiː/ everywhere | /ðə/ | Relax, use schwa in unstressed positions |

### Correction Practice Methods

**Method 1: Isolation Drill**
- Practice problem sound ALONE (e.g., just /θ/ sound)
- Add to simple word: "th... th... think"
- Use in sentence: "I think... that..."

**Method 2: Minimal Pair Contrast**
- Practice contrasting pairs: "sink/think", "men/man", "lice/rice"
- Record both, listen for difference
- Repeat until you can hear AND produce the difference

**Method 3: Physical Positioning**
- Use mirror to see mouth position
- Use hand gestures to cue tongue position
- Feel the physical difference between correct and incorrect

### 🔧 Common Pitfalls & Fixes

| Pitfall | Coach's Fix |
|---------|-------------|
| **Stopping after Part 1 mistake** | Keep going! The exam doesn't stop, and neither should you |
| **Speaking too quietly** | Pretend you're talking to someone across a table |
| **Running out of things in Part 2** | Add details: when, where, who with, why memorable, how you felt |
| **Giving 1-sentence Part 3 answers** | Always ask yourself "Why?" and give examples |
| **Checking timer while speaking** | Glance at it during preparation only |
| **Apologizing for mistakes** | Never apologize—just continue speaking |

### 🎯 Pro Tips

1. **The Part 2 note-taking trick**: Write 3-4 keywords only, not full sentences
2. **The Part 3 structure**: Opinion → Reason → Example → Extension
3. **If your mind goes blank**: Say "That's an interesting question, let me think..." and buy yourself 2-3 seconds
4. **Record everything**: You'll be surprised what you notice when you listen back

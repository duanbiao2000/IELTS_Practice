## 🎯 GUIDED PRACTICE

### Task Overview
Complete your second full mock exam with realistic timing and continuous recording, then save for comparison with Mock ①.

### Step-by-Step Instructions

#### STEP 1: Set Up Your Testing Space [2 min]
- Quiet room with no distractions
- Recording device ready (test audio)
- Timer visible
- Pen and paper ready (Part 2 notes only)
- Water nearby (optional)

#### STEP 2: Choose Your Test Prompts

**Part 1 Questions (DIFFERENT from Mock ①):**

*Set A: Hobbies & Free Time*
1. What do you do in your free time?
2. How did you become interested in this?
3. Is it a popular hobby in your country?

*Set B: Food & Cooking*
1. What's your favorite food?
2. Can you cook? What dishes can you make?
3. Do you prefer eating at home or in restaurants?

**Part 2 Topic Card:**

```
Describe a skill you would like to learn.
You should say:
- What the skill is
- Why you want to learn it
- How you would learn it
- And explain why this skill is important to you.
```

**Part 3 Discussion Questions:**
1. What skills are most important for young people to learn today?
2. How has technology changed the way people learn new skills?
3. Do you think schools should teach practical skills like cooking or budgeting?
4. Is it ever too late to learn a new skill?

#### STEP 3: Run the Mock Exam [11-14 min]

**Timeline:**
```
START RECORDING NOW

[0:00-4:00]   Part 1: Answer examiner questions
[4:00-5:00]   Part 2: 1 minute preparation (make notes)
[5:00-7:00]   Part 2: 2 minutes speaking
[7:00-11:00]  Part 3: Discussion questions

STOP RECORDING
```

**During the exam, consciously apply:**
- ✅ Weak forms where appropriate
- ✅ Replacement phrases instead of "um/uh"
- ✅ Extended answers in Part 3 (3+ sentences)
- ✅ Clear pronunciation (especially problem sounds you practiced)
- ✅ Natural intonation (not flat)

#### STEP 4: Save Your Recording [1 min]
Label your file: `mock-002-[date].m4a` or `.mp3`
Do NOT listen to it yet—that's Task 65!

## 📝 MODEL ANSWER & REFERENCE

### What Changes Between Mock ① and Mock ②

Students typically see improvement in:

| Area | Mock ① Typical | Mock ② Typical | Why |
|------|----------------|----------------|-----|
| **Comfort** | Nervous, unfamiliar | More comfortable | Experience helps |
| **Fluency** | Some hesitation | Smoother delivery | Practice pays off |
| **Fillers** | Many "um/uh" | Fewer fillers | Replacement phrases work |
| **Part 2 length** | Often under 2 min | Closer to 2 min | Better pacing awareness |
| **Part 3 depth** | Brief answers | Extended answers | Structure training helps |

### Sample Part 2 Response (Skill Topic - 2-minute structure)

> "I'd like to talk about a skill I've been wanting to learn for quite some time now, which is conversational Spanish.
>
> The reason I want to learn Spanish is primarily for travel and personal growth. I've always been fascinated by Latin American culture, and I believe that being able to speak the language would open up a completely different dimension of travel experiences. **From my perspective**, when you can communicate with locals in their own language, you gain insights into their culture that you simply can't get as a tourist.
>
> As for how I would learn it, I plan to take a multi-faceted approach. **Actually**, I've already downloaded several language learning apps like Duolingo and Babbel, which are great for building vocabulary. However, I realize that apps alone aren't enough, so I'm planning to find a language exchange partner online—someone who wants to practice English in exchange for helping me with Spanish. **Ideally**, I'd also like to take evening classes once or twice a week for more structured learning.
>
> This skill is important to me for several reasons. **First and foremost**, I believe learning a new language keeps your mind sharp and has been shown to have cognitive benefits. Additionally, Spanish is spoken by over 500 million people worldwide, so it would significantly expand my ability to communicate globally. **What's more**, the process of learning a language teaches you patience, persistence, and cultural humility—qualities that I think are valuable in all areas of life.
>
> **Overall**, while I know it will be challenging, I'm genuinely excited about the prospect of becoming conversational in Spanish over the next couple of years."

### Sample Part 3 Response (Extended Answer Structure)

**Q: What skills are most important for young people to learn today?**

> "**That's an interesting question**, and I think there are several critical skills that young people need to succeed in today's world. **Well**, perhaps the most important is digital literacy—the ability to navigate, evaluate, and create content using digital technologies. **This is increasingly essential** because almost every career now involves some level of digital competency.
>
> **Furthermore**, I believe critical thinking skills are more important than ever. **In an age of information overload**, young people need to be able to distinguish reliable information from misinformation, and to think independently rather than simply accepting what they see on social media.
>
> **Another crucial skill** is adaptability. **The job market is changing rapidly** due to automation and artificial intelligence, so the ability to learn new skills and adapt to new circumstances is invaluable. **From my perspective**, the ability to reinvent yourself professionally will be one of the most valuable skills in the coming decades.
>
> **Having said that**, I also believe that interpersonal skills and emotional intelligence shouldn't be overlooked. **While technical skills are important**, the ability to work well with others, communicate effectively, and understand people from different backgrounds is what often distinguishes truly successful people from those who are merely technically proficient."

### Strategies Applied in These Model Answers

| Strategy | Where It Appears | Benefit |
|----------|------------------|---------|
| **Replacement phrases** | "That's an interesting question," "From my perspective" | No fillers, professional |
| **Extended structure** | All answers are 4+ sentences | Shows development |
| **Complex sentences** | "While technical skills are important..." | Grammatical range |
| **Linking words** | "Furthermore," "Having said that," "Overall" | Coherence and cohesion |
| **Weak forms** | In function words (implied in pronunciation) | Natural rhythm |

## 💡 COACH'S NOTES

### 🎓 Teaching Points

**1. Mock Exams Show Real Progress**
It's hard to feel progress day-to-day. But comparing Mock ① and Mock ② (which you'll do in Task 65) gives you objective evidence of improvement. This is motivating!

**2. Familiarity Breeds Confidence**
The second time you do anything, you feel more comfortable. This mock exam should feel less intimidating than the first. That confidence boost is real progress.

**3. Apply What You've Practiced**
This week, you've worked on:
- Weak forms (Tasks 55-56)
- Filler replacement (Task 60)
- Extended Part 3 answers (Task 61)
- Pronunciation (ELSA tasks)

Consciously use these tools during the mock exam. They're not just exercises—they're real skills now.

### 🔧 Common Pitfalls & Fixes

| Pitfall | Coach's Fix |
|---------|-------------|
| **Comparing to perfection** | Compare to Mock ①, not to perfection. Progress matters! |
| **Forgetting to apply Week 3 skills** | Before starting, remind yourself: weak forms, no um/uh, extended answers |
| **Getting discouraged by mistakes** | Mistakes are data. They show what to work on in Week 4 |
| **Not taking it seriously** | Treat this like the real exam. The practice value is in the simulation |
| **Listening to recording immediately** | Wait for Task 65. You need objectivity from time passing |

### 🎯 Pro Tips

1. **Simulate exam conditions**: No phone, no distractions, same environment you'll use on test day
2. **Use the exact timing**: Part 2 gets exactly 1 minute prep, 2 minutes speak
3. **Apply ALL your strategies**: This is a dress rehearsal for the real thing
4. **Note your feelings**: How you felt during the exam matters as much as how you performed

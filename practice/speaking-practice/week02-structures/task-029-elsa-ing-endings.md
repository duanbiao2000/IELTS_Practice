# Task 29: ELSA -ing Endings

## 📚 PRE-STUDY [8 minutes]

### Before You Begin
**Why are -ing endings so important?**

The -ing sound is one of the **most frequently mispronounced sounds** in English:
- It appears in present continuous tense (I am learning)
- It appears in gerunds (I enjoy reading)
- It appears in adjectives (It's interesting)
- Swallowing this sound makes your speech unclear

### Key Concepts

**The Correct -ing Sound:**
```
/ɪŋ/ = short 'i' + 'ng' sound (NOT 'in'!)
```

| Common Mistake | Correct | Why It Matters |
|----------------|---------|----------------|
| "readin'" | reading | "in" vs "ing" changes meaning |
| "walkin'" | walking | Dropped 'g' sounds informal |
| "work-en" | working | Don't add extra sounds |

**The -ing Sound Position:**
- ✅ /ɪŋ/ - clear 'ng' sound (reading, walking, working)
- ❌ /ɪn/ - sounds like "in" (very common mistake)
- ❌ /ɪk/ - adding a 'k' sound (some regional accents)

## 🎯 GUIDED PRACTICE

### Task Overview
Use ELSA to practice 10 words with -ing endings, focusing on NOT swallowing the sound.

### Step-by-Step Instructions

#### STEP 1: Understand the Sound [2 min]

**Physical Position:**
1. Start with the short 'i' sound (/ɪ/) - like in "bit"
2. Move your tongue to the back of your mouth
3. The 'ng' sound comes from your **nose**, not your throat!
4. **Do NOT** close with a 'k' or 'n' sound

**Try this:**
- Say "sing" - hold the ending. Feel the vibration in your nose.
- Say "ring" - same nose vibration.
- This is the /ŋ/ sound you want for -ing endings.

## 💡 COACH'S NOTES

### 🎓 Teaching Points

**1. The 'ng' is a Nasal Sound**
- The /ŋ/ sound is made by pushing air through your **nose**
- Touch your nose while saying "sing" - you'll feel vibration!
- This is different from /n/ (nose vibration + tongue on roof)
- This is different from /k/ (throat sound)

**2. Why Learners Drop the 'g'**
- In fast speech, native speakers DO soften -ing (sounds like "in")
- BUT: The sound is still /ɪŋ/, not /ɪn/
- Learners drop it completely → sounds like a different word
- **Rule:** Learners should pronounce clearly, even if natives soften

**3. When -ing is Most Important**
- At the **end of sentences** (don't trail off)
- Before **pauses** (give the sound its full time)
- In **important words** (content words like learning, reading)

### 🔧 Common Pitfalls & Fixes

| Pitfall | Example | Coach's Fix |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Dropping the 'g' completely** | "I'm readin'" | Practice: "read-ING" - exaggerate the ending |
| **Adding a 'k' sound** | "work-kin" | The 'ng' has no 'k' - just nasal sound |
| **Converting to 'in'** | "something" → "somethin'" | Focus: nose vibration, not tongue |
| **Rushing through the word** | Mumbled -ing | Slow down! Give -ing its full time |

### 🎯 Pro Tips

1. **The Nose Test**: Touch your nose when saying -ing words. Feel vibration?
2. **Exaggerate first**: Over-pronounce -ing endings, then naturalize
3. **Record and compare**: Record yourself saying "reading" vs. a native speaker
4. **Daily practice**: Pick 5 -ing words per day to focus on

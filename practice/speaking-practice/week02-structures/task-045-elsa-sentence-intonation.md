# Task 45: ELSA - Sentence Intonation (Questions vs. Statements)

## 📚 PRE-STUDY [10 minutes]

### Before You Begin

**What is Sentence Intonation?**

Intonation is the **rise and fall of your voice** as you speak. It's the "music" of English.

**Why Intonation Matters:**
- It changes meaning (statement vs. question)
- It shows attitude (certainty, surprise, interest)
- It helps listeners follow your speech
- It makes you sound natural or robotic

### Key Concepts

**The Two Main Patterns:**

| Pattern | Name | Pitch Movement | Example |
|---------|------|----------------|---------|
| **Falling** | Statements | Starts higher, ends lower | "I live in Beijing." |
| **Rising** | Yes/No Questions | Starts lower, ends higher | "Do you like Beijing?" |

**Wh- Questions (What, Where, When, Why, How):**
- Usually **falling** at the end (like statements)
- "Where do you live?" (voice goes down)

**Yes/No Questions:**
- Always **rising** at the end
- "Do you like coffee?" (voice goes up)

**The Visual:**
```
Statement:      I  live  in  Beijing.
                  ↗                ↘

Yes/No Question:  Do  you  like  Beijing?
                    ↘                ↗

Wh- Question:     Where  do  you  live?
                    ↗              ↘
```

## 📝 MODEL ANSWER & REFERENCE

### Complete Intonation Patterns

| Sentence Type | End Intonation | Example |
|---------------|----------------|---------|
| **Statement** | Falling ↘ | "I live in Beijing." |
| **Command** | Falling ↘ | "Sit down." |
| **Wh- question** | Falling ↘ | "Where do you live?" |
| **Yes/No question** | Rising ↗ | "Do you like Beijing?" |
| **Choice question** | Rising ↗ then falling ↘ | "Do you want tea or coffee?" |
| **Question tag** | Rising ↗ (uncertain) or falling ↘ (certain) | "You're coming, aren't you?" |
| **List** | Rising ↗ ↗ then falling ↘ | "I bought apples, pears, and bananas." |

### Common Intonation Mistakes

| Mistake | What It Sounds Like | Why It's Wrong |
|---------|---------------------|----------------|
| **Flat intonation** | "I live in Beijing." (no change) | Sounds robotic, bored, or uninterested |
| **Rising on statements** | "I live in Beijing↗" | Sounds like you're unsure or asking a question |
| **Falling on yes/no questions** | "Do you like Beijing↘" | Sounds rude or abrupt |
| **Over-exaggeration** | "I LIVE in BEI-JING↘↘↘" | Sounds sarcastic or unnatural |

### IELTS Speaking Intonation Tips

| IELTS Situation | Recommended Intonation |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| **Part 1: Answering questions** | Mostly falling (confident statements) |
| **Part 1: Confirming** | Slight rise ("Yes, I do↗") |
| **Part 2: Telling a story** | Mix: falling for statements, slight rise for continuing |
| **Part 2: Building excitement** | Higher pitch overall, more variation |
| **Part 3: Discussing ideas** | Falling (confident) but with more pitch variety |
| **Part 3: Showing uncertainty** | Slight rise ("I'm not entirely sure↗") |

## 💡 COACH'S NOTES

### 🎓 Teaching Points

**1. Why Your Intonation Might Be Wrong**

**Chinese intonation:**
- Uses pitch changes to change word meaning (tones)
- Sentence-level pitch changes are less important
- Statements and questions can have similar intonation

**English intonation:**
- Uses pitch changes to show sentence type and attitude
- Statement vs. question intonation is CRUCIAL
- Wrong intonation = wrong meaning!

**2. The "Uncertainty" Rising Intonation**

Sometimes statements end with a slight rise when:
- You're uncertain: "I think it's around 5 o'clock↗"
- You're checking: "You have the tickets↗"
- You're continuing: "I went to the store, and then↗..."

This is natural! The key is CONTEXT.

**3. How to Hear Your Own Intonation**

**Methods:**
1. **Record yourself**—listen back and draw the pitch line
2. **Use ELSA**—it shows pitch visually
3. **Exaggerate first**—make the rise/fall very obvious, then tone down
4. **Mimic native speakers**—copy their pitch exactly

### 🔧 Common Pitfalls & Fixes

| Pitfall | Example | Coach's Fix |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Always going up at the end** | "I live in Beijing↗. I'm a student↗." | This is called "upspeak" and sounds uncertain. Practice ending low. |
| **Never going up** | "Do you like Beijing↘?" | Sounds abrupt or rude. Yes/no questions MUST go up. |
| **Flat monotone** | All sentences same pitch | Vary your pitch more—exaggerate at first! |
| **Only using middle pitch** | No variation at all | English uses wide pitch range—stretch your voice! |
| **Intonation doesn't match meaning** | "I'm SO excited↘" (bored tone) | Match your pitch to your emotion! |

### 🎯 Pro Tips

1. **The "humming technique"**—hum your sentence to feel the pitch changes
2. **Hand gestures**—move your hand up/down while speaking to visualize pitch
3. **Exaggerate for practice**—make the rise/fall VERY obvious, then naturalize
4. **Record and draw**—listen to your recording and draw the pitch line
5. **Mimic everything**—copy native speakers' intonation, not just their words

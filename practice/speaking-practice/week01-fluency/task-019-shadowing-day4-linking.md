# Task 19: Shadowing Day 4 - Focus on Linking Sounds

## 📚 PRE-STUDY [5 minutes]

### Before You Begin

**What Are Linking Sounds?**

Native speakers don't say words separately—they **link** them together. This is called "connected speech."

**Example:**
- Written: "turn it on"
- How it sounds: "tur-ni-ton" (three words flow as one)

**Why Linking Matters:**

| Without Linking | With Linking |
|-----------------|--------------|
| Sounds choppy, robotic | Sounds smooth, natural |
| Hard to follow | Easy to understand |
| Lower fluency score | Higher fluency score |
| Tiring to speak | Efficient, flowing |

### Key Concepts

| Type of Linking | Description | Example |
|----------------|-------------|---------|
| **Consonant + Vowel** | Final consonant links to next vowel | "turn_it" |
| **Vowel + Vowel** | Extra /j/ or /w/ sound appears | "see_it" → "see-yit" |
| **Same Consonants** | Sound is held longer | "bad_dog" |
| ** disappearing t** | /t/ disappears between consonants | "next_day" → "nex_day" |

### Common Linking Patterns to Notice Today

**1. Consonant → Vowel (most common)**
- "turn it on" → /tɜːr nɪ tɒn/
- "get out" → /ge taʊt/
- "pick it up" → /pɪ kɪ tʌp/

**2. "The" → /ðə/ before consonant, /ði/ before vowel**
- "the book" → /ðə bʊk/
- "the apple" → /ði æpl/

**3. "To" → /tə/ (weak form)**
- "turn to" → /tɜːn tə/
- "go to" → /ɡəʊ tə/

| Word | Before Consonant | Before Vowel |
|------|------------------|--------------|

| **to** | /tə/ "go to sleep" | /tu/ "go to it" |

**3. Common Linking Phrases**

| Phrase | With Linking |
|--------|--------------|
| "I want to" | "I wan-ta" |

| "give it to me" | "gi-vi-ta-mi" |

### Linking Rules Reference

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **C + V** | Consonant at end + vowel at start = link | "turn_it" |
| **V + V** | Vowel + vowel = /j/ or /w/ appears | "see_it" = /siː jɪt/ |
| **r + V** | Add /r/ sound (British) | "law_and" = /lɔː rənd/ |
| **Same C** | Same consonant = hold longer | "bad_dog" |
| **t between vowels** | Quick /t/ or American flap | "better" = /beə/ |

## 💡 COACH'S NOTES

### 🎓 Teaching Points

**1. Why Linking Feels Unnatural**
> In written English, words are separated by spaces.
> Your brain sees "turn it on" as THREE words.
> But in spoken English, it's ONE sound stream.
>
> Unlearning the space between words takes practice.
> Shadowing with linking focus is the best way to retrain.

**2. The "Tur-ni-ton" Principle**
"turn it on" = three words
"tur-ni-ton" = one connected sound

Native speakers don't say "turn... it... on" with three separate sounds.
They say "tur-ni-ton" as if it's one word.

**Practice: Say "tunnitonna" fast. Now say "turn it on" with the same flow.**

**3. Weak Forms**
Small words like "to," "for," "at," "and" have weak forms:
- to → /tə/ (not /tuː/)
- for → /fə/ (not /fɔː/)
- at → /ət/ (not /æt/)
- and → /ən/ or /ənd/ (not /ænd/)

**Using weak forms = sounding like a native speaker.**

### 🔧 Common Pitfalls & Fixes

| Pitfall | Problem | Coach's Fix |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Speaking each word separately** | Robotic, choppy | Focus on connecting C+V patterns |
| **Over-linking (mumbling)** | Unclear, hard to understand | Link but keep clarity |
| **Not linking at all** | Unnatural, stressed | Practice C+V patterns deliberately |
| **Linking the same way in writing** | Confuses spelling vs. speech | Remember: linking is SPEECH only |
| **Giving up too soon** | Linking feels weird at first | It takes 2-3 weeks to feel natural |

### 🎯 Pro Tips

1. **Start with C+V** - master consonant-to-vowel links first
2. **Use slow motion** - practice linking very slowly, then speed up
3. **Record and compare** - record linked vs. unlinked, hear the difference
4. **Focus on phrases** - practice common phrases, not isolated words
5. **Listen to fast speech** - native speakers link the most in casual speech

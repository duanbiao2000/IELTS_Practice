## Reading Passage 1

### The Evolution of the Internet

**A.** The Internet began as a United States government research project in the late 1960s. Originally called ARPANET, it was designed to create a decentralized communication network that could survive a nuclear attack. The system connected four universities: UCLA, Stanford Research Institute, UC Santa Barbara, and the University of Utah. By 1971, the network had expanded to 15 nodes and was being used for email and file sharing.

**B.** The development of TCP/IP (Transmission Control Protocol/Internet Protocol) in the 1970s was a crucial breakthrough. This standardized method for transmitting data allowed different networks to connect with each other, forming the basis of the modern Internet. In 1983, ARPANET officially adopted TCP/IP, marking the birth of the Internet as we know it today. The protocol's success was due to its simplicity, reliability, and ability to work across different hardware systems.

**C.** The World Wide Web, invented by Tim Berners-Lee in 1989 at CERN, transformed the Internet from a tool for researchers into a platform accessible to everyone. Berners-Lee developed three key technologies: HTML (HyperText Markup Language) for creating web pages, HTTP (HyperText Transfer Protocol) for transmitting them, and URLs (Uniform Resource Locators) for identifying resources. These innovations made navigation intuitive and content sharing simple.

**D.** The launch of the Mosaic web browser in 1993 marked the beginning of the Internet's mainstream adoption. Unlike previous browsers, Mosaic displayed images alongside text, making the web visually appealing and easier to use. Within a year, web traffic increased by 341,000%. The commercial potential became clear, and companies rushed to establish an online presence. The late 1990s saw the dot-com boom and bust, a period of rapid growth followed by a dramatic crash.

**E.** The early 2000s brought Web 2.0, characterized by user-generated content and social interaction. Platforms like MySpace, Facebook, and YouTube enabled ordinary people to create and share content without technical expertise. This democratization of publishing transformed media consumption patterns and created new business models. Advertising revenue shifted from traditional media to online platforms, and social media became a primary source of news and entertainment for millions.

**F.** Today's Internet continues to evolve rapidly. Mobile devices have surpassed desktop computers as the primary access point, and emerging technologies like 5G networks promise faster speeds and more reliable connections. Artificial intelligence powers personalized recommendations, voice assistants, and autonomous systems. The Internet of Things connects everyday objects—from refrigerators to cars—to the network, creating new possibilities and security challenges.

**G.** Despite its benefits, the Internet faces significant challenges. Issues of digital divide persist, with unequal access based on geography, income, and education. Privacy concerns have grown as companies collect vast amounts of personal data. Misinformation spreads quickly through social networks, threatening democratic processes. Cybersecurity threats become more sophisticated as criminals exploit the network's complexity. These problems require coordinated solutions from governments, technology companies, and civil society.

## Reading Passage 2

### The Psychology of Decision-Making

**A.** Decision-making is a fundamental cognitive process that influences every aspect of human life, from trivial daily choices to life-altering decisions. The brain employs various strategies to process information and reach conclusions, often relying on mental shortcuts known as heuristics. While heuristics generally serve us well by enabling quick decisions, they can also lead to systematic biases and errors in judgment.

**B.** Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky's research on prospect theory revolutionized our understanding of how people make decisions under uncertainty. They demonstrated that individuals weigh potential losses more heavily than equivalent gains—a phenomenon called loss aversion. For example, people feel the pain of losing $100 more intensely than the pleasure of gaining $100, which often leads to risk-averse behavior when protecting gains and risk-seeking behavior when trying to avoid losses.

**C.** The availability heuristic is another cognitive shortcut that affects judgment. People estimate the likelihood of events based on how easily examples come to mind. Media coverage that focuses on dramatic but rare events, such as plane crashes or shark attacks, can distort perceptions of risk. This explains why many people overestimate the danger of unlikely events while underestimating common threats like heart disease or car accidents.

**D.** Confirmation bias leads people to seek information that confirms their existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence. This tendency is amplified by the modern information environment, where algorithms personalize content based on past behavior. Social media platforms create echo chambers where users are primarily exposed to opinions that reinforce their views, potentially polarizing society and making constructive dialogue more difficult.

**E.** The sunk cost fallacy describes the tendency to continue investing in a project or course of action because of previous investments, even when abandoning it would be more rational. This bias explains why people stay in unsatisfying relationships, persist with failing business ventures, or continue watching disappointing movies. The emotional attachment to past investments clouds rational assessment of future prospects.

**F.** Decision fatigue occurs when the quality of decisions deteriorates after making many choices. The mental energy required for decision-making is a finite resource that gets depleted throughout the day. This explains why people often make poor food choices in the evening or make impulsive purchases when tired after a day of shopping. Successful individuals often reduce decision fatigue by establishing routines and automating trivial choices.

**G.** Understanding these cognitive biases is the first step toward improving decision quality. Strategies include seeking diverse perspectives, deliberately considering contradictory evidence, using structured decision frameworks, and implementing cooling-off periods before important choices. Organizations can design decision processes that minimize bias through group decision-making, devil's advocacy, and pre-mortem analysis. By recognizing the limitations of human judgment, individuals and institutions can make better choices.

## Reading Passage 3

### The Cultural Significance of Masks

**A.** Masks are among humanity's oldest and most universal art forms, appearing in cultures worldwide for thousands of years. From ancient Egyptian burial masks to contemporary theater masks, these objects serve diverse functions: religious rituals, artistic expression, social commentary, and practical purposes. The use of masks reveals fundamental aspects of human psychology, social organization, and cultural values.

**B.** Ritual masks have deep roots in religious and spiritual practices. Many indigenous cultures believe masks can transform the wearer into a deity, ancestor, or spirit. In African traditions, ritual masks are often associated with secret societies and used in coming-of-age ceremonies, funerals, and agricultural festivals. The mask is not merely a costume but a sacred object embodying spiritual power. The transformation is considered real and meaningful within the cultural context, not symbolic or theatrical.

**C.** In Asian cultures, particularly Japan and China, masks play crucial roles in traditional theater. Noh theater, developed in 14th century Japan, uses stylized masks to represent characters such as ghosts, demons, and elderly men. The masks are designed with subtly shifting expressions that change as the performer moves and the lighting changes. Chinese opera masks use color symbolism to indicate character traits—red for bravery, blue for strength, white for treachery, black for justice. These theatrical traditions preserve ancient stories and moral lessons while providing entertainment.

**D.** Carnival masks have a different social function. In Venice, elaborate masks allowed people of all social classes to mingle anonymously during Carnival, temporarily breaking down social barriers. This tradition dates back to the 13th century and continues today. Similarly, Mardi Gras masks in New Orleans and carnival celebrations in Brazil use masks to create a sense of collective anonymity and freedom. The mask becomes a tool for social inversion, allowing individuals to temporarily step outside their everyday identities.

**E.** Medical masks represent a more practical application. The earliest recorded use of surgical masks dates to the late 19th century, when Polish surgeon Jan Mikulicz-Radecki introduced gauze masks during surgery to prevent infection. Modern medical masks serve both protective and symbolic functions. During the COVID-19 pandemic, masks became visible symbols of community responsibility and solidarity. However, they also became politicized, with debates over mandatory masking reflecting deeper cultural conflicts about individual rights versus public health.

**F.** The psychology behind mask-wearing is complex. For the wearer, a mask can provide protection but also a sense of anonymity that reduces inhibitions. The "disinhibition effect" explains why people may behave more boldly or aggressively when masked—a phenomenon relevant to both masked protests and anonymous online behavior. For the observer, masks conceal facial expressions that typically communicate emotion, making interaction more challenging. This can lead to mistrust and uncertainty, particularly in cultures where direct eye contact and facial cues are important.

**G.** Contemporary artists continue to use masks as powerful tools for commentary on identity, surveillance, and modern life. Digital masks in social media and virtual reality represent the evolution of this ancient concept into new forms. The persistence of masks across cultures and centuries speaks to their fundamental role in human experience. Whether hiding or revealing, connecting or separating, masks help us navigate the complex relationship between individual identity and social belonging.

## Vocabulary & Concepts

### Passage 1: The Evolution of the Internet
- **Decentralized**: Not controlled by a central authority
- **Protocol**: A set of rules governing data exchange
- **Democratization**: Making something accessible to everyone
- **Digital divide**: The gap between those with and without Internet access
- **Internet of Things**: The network of connected physical objects

### Passage 2: The Psychology of Decision-Making
- **Heuristic**: Mental shortcut for problem-solving
- **Loss aversion**: The tendency to prefer avoiding losses over acquiring gains
- **Confirmation bias**: Seeking information that confirms existing beliefs
- **Sunk cost fallacy**: Continuing investment due to past expenditures
- **Decision fatigue**: Deteriorating decision quality after multiple choices

### Passage 3: The Cultural Significance of Masks

- **Transformation**: A complete change in form or appearance
- **Social inversion**: Reversing normal social order or roles
- **Anonymity**: The state of being unidentified
- **Disinhibition**: Loss of restraint or inhibition

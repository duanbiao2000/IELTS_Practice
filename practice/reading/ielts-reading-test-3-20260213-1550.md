## Passage 1

### The History of Coffee

**A.** Coffee, the world's most popular beverage after water, has a rich and complex history spanning over a millennium. Legend has it that an Ethiopian goatherd named Kaldi discovered coffee around the 9th century when he noticed his goats became unusually energetic after consuming berries from a certain plant. Fascinated by this phenomenon, Kaldi tried the berries himself and experienced similar stimulating effects. Monks at a local monastery soon learned of this discovery and began using the berries to stay awake during long hours of prayer and meditation.

**B.** From Ethiopia, coffee cultivation spread to the Arabian Peninsula, particularly Yemen, by the 15th century. Coffee houses, known as qahveh khaneh, began appearing in cities across the Middle East. These establishments became important social and political hubs where people gathered to drink coffee, play chess, discuss news, and exchange ideas. The popularity of coffee led to its being called "the wine of Araby." Arabian merchants carefully guarded their monopoly on coffee cultivation, exporting only roasted beans to prevent foreigners from growing their own crops.

**C.** By the 17th century, coffee had reached Europe through Venetian merchants. Initially met with suspicion by some religious leaders who considered it a "bitter invention of Satan," coffee eventually gained widespread acceptance after Pope Clement VIII tasted it and declared it delicious. Coffee houses proliferated throughout Europe, becoming centers of intellectual and political discourse. In London, coffee houses were dubbed "penny universities" because for the price of a penny, one could engage in stimulating conversations with educated individuals.

**D.** The Dutch played a crucial role in spreading coffee cultivation beyond the Arabian monopoly. In the late 17th century, they successfully smuggled coffee plants to their colonies in the East Indies, particularly Java, which became synonymous with coffee. The French, not to be outdone, obtained coffee plants and established plantations in the Caribbean, most notably in Martinique. From these islands, coffee cultivation spread to Central and South America, with Brazil eventually becoming the world's largest coffee producer.

**E.** The invention of the espresso machine in Italy in the late 19th century revolutionized coffee consumption. In 1884, Angelo Moriondo of Turin patented the first espresso machine, which forced hot water through finely ground coffee under high pressure. This innovation created a concentrated, flavorful coffee that could be prepared quickly, catering to the fast-paced lifestyle of modern cities. The espresso machine's design continued to evolve, with improvements by Luigi Bezzera and Desiderio Pavoni making it more practical for commercial use.

**F.** In the 20th century, coffee consumption patterns diversified significantly. The rise of instant coffee, developed by Japanese-American chemist Satori Kato in 1901, made coffee preparation more convenient for households. The specialty coffee movement emerged in the 1970s, emphasizing high-quality beans, precise roasting techniques, and artisanal preparation methods. This trend accelerated with the establishment of companies like Starbucks, which popularized coffee-based beverages beyond traditional black coffee.

**G.** Today, coffee production involves over 25 million farmers in more than 50 countries, primarily in the tropical "Bean Belt." Coffee beans are actually the seeds of coffee cherries, which are harvested by hand or machine, processed, dried, and roasted to develop their characteristic flavor. The two most commercially important species are Arabica and Robusta. Arabica beans, favored for their superior quality and complex flavors, constitute approximately 60% of global production, while Robusta beans, known for their higher caffeine content and bitter taste, account for the remaining 40%.

**H.** The environmental impact of coffee cultivation has become an increasing concern. Traditional shade-grown coffee, cultivated under native tree canopies, supports biodiversity and reduces the need for chemical inputs. However, many farms have converted to sun-grown varieties that yield more beans but require more pesticides and fertilizers, leading to deforestation and soil degradation. Climate change poses another threat, as rising temperatures and changing rainfall patterns affect coffee-growing regions, potentially reducing suitable cultivation areas by up to 50% by 2050.

**I.** Coffee culture continues to evolve, with new trends constantly emerging. Cold brew coffee, steeped in cold water for extended periods, has gained popularity for its smooth, less acidic taste. Nitro coffee, infused with nitrogen gas, mimics the creamy texture of stout beer. Meanwhile, concerns about sustainability have led to increased demand for fair trade and organic certifications, ensuring farmers receive fair prices and environmentally friendly practices are followed. The journey from goat herder's discovery to global beverage demonstrates coffee's remarkable ability to adapt and thrive across cultures and centuries.

## Passage 2

### The Evolution of Sleep

**A.** Sleep, a seemingly simple daily activity, has puzzled scientists and philosophers for millennia. Ancient Egyptians believed sleep involved traveling to the afterlife, while Aristotle proposed that sleep resulted from the accumulation of vapors in the brain. Throughout history, sleep was often viewed merely as a passive state where the body and mind rested. Only in the last century have researchers begun to understand sleep as an active, complex biological process essential for health and survival.

**B.** The invention of the electroencephalograph (EEG) in the 1920s revolutionized sleep research. This device measures electrical activity in the brain, revealing that sleep consists of distinct cycles with different brain wave patterns. In the 1950s, researchers identified two main sleep stages: Rapid Eye Movement (REM) sleep, characterized by rapid eye movements and vivid dreaming, and Non-REM (NREM) sleep, which includes three progressively deeper stages. A typical adult completes 4-6 sleep cycles per night, each lasting approximately 90 minutes.

**C.** During NREM sleep, particularly in the deeper stages (N3), the body performs critical restorative functions. Growth hormones are released, promoting tissue repair and muscle growth. The immune system strengthens its defenses against infections and diseases. Meanwhile, the brain consolidates memories, transferring information from short-term to long-term storage. This process explains why sleep deprivation severely impairs learning and memory formation—without adequate sleep, the brain cannot effectively process and retain new information.

**D.** REM sleep, occupying about 20-25% of adult sleep time, serves different but equally important functions. During REM sleep, the brain processes emotional experiences and integrates them into existing memory networks. This helps explain why dreams often incorporate recent events and emotional themes. REM sleep also appears crucial for cognitive flexibility, enabling the brain to make novel connections between seemingly unrelated concepts. Many creative insights and problem-solving breakthroughs occur after REM-rich sleep periods.

**E.** Sleep requirements vary significantly across the lifespan. Newborns sleep 14-17 hours per day, gradually decreasing to 7-9 hours by adulthood. Elderly individuals often experience fragmented sleep and reduced total sleep time, partly due to changes in circadian rhythms and medical conditions. Interestingly, individual variations exist even within age groups—some people are genetically predisposed to require less sleep while others need more, challenging the notion of a universal sleep requirement.

**F.** Sleep deprivation has profound consequences on physical and mental health. Chronic sleep loss increases the risk of obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and weakened immune function. The cognitive impacts include impaired attention, reduced working memory capacity, and slower reaction times. One study showed that after 17-19 hours without sleep, cognitive performance becomes equivalent to someone with a blood alcohol concentration of 0.05%, legally impaired in many jurisdictions.

**G.** Modern lifestyle factors have contributed to widespread sleep deprivation. Artificial light, particularly blue light from electronic devices, suppresses melatonin production and delays sleep onset. Irregular work schedules, including night shifts and early morning starts, disrupt the body's natural circadian rhythm. The "always-on" culture of constant connectivity and information availability leads to psychological arousal that interferes with relaxation and sleep onset. Consequently, sleep disorders such as insomnia have become increasingly prevalent, affecting approximately 30% of adults at some point.

**H.** Despite growing awareness of sleep's importance, misconceptions persist. Many believe they can "catch up" on missed sleep during weekends, but research shows that sleep debt cannot be fully recovered through extended sleep periods. Another myth suggests that older adults need less sleep; in reality, they may still require 7-8 hours but have difficulty maintaining sleep. Perhaps most dangerous is the belief that functioning on minimal sleep demonstrates strength and productivity, when in reality it significantly impairs performance and health.

**I.** Improving sleep hygiene—the set of practices promoting quality sleep—can yield significant benefits. Establishing a consistent sleep schedule, even on weekends, helps regulate the body's internal clock. Creating a cool, dark, and quiet sleep environment minimizes disruptions. Avoiding caffeine, alcohol, and large meals close to bedtime reduces sleep fragmentation. Limiting screen time before bed allows natural melatonin production. Regular exercise, particularly in the morning or afternoon, promotes deeper sleep at night, though vigorous activity too close to bedtime should be avoided.

**J.** The future of sleep science holds promising developments. Wearable devices and smartphone apps increasingly monitor sleep patterns, providing personalized insights and recommendations. Chronotherapy—treatments timed to circadian rhythms—may optimize medication effectiveness for various conditions. Gene therapies might eventually address genetic sleep disorders. However, the simplest yet most challenging intervention remains prioritizing sleep as a fundamental pillar of health, alongside nutrition and physical activity. As research continues to unravel sleep's mysteries, one truth remains clear: sleep is not a luxury but a biological necessity.

## Passage 3

### The Architecture of Sustainable Cities

**A.** As the global population becomes increasingly urbanized, with over 68% expected to live in cities by 2050, the environmental impact of urban areas has become a critical concern. Cities currently consume 78% of the world's energy and produce more than 60% of greenhouse gas emissions, despite occupying only 2% of the Earth's land surface. Traditional urban planning models, designed for automobile-centric development and resource-intensive lifestyles, are proving unsustainable in the face of climate change and resource depletion. Consequently, architects, urban planners, and policymakers are reimagining cities through the lens of sustainability.

**B.** Biophilic design, which seeks to incorporate nature into built environments, has emerged as a key principle of sustainable architecture. This approach recognizes that humans have an innate biological need to connect with nature, as articulated by biologist E.O. Wilson's "biophilia hypothesis." Biophilic elements include living walls covered in vegetation, indoor gardens, natural ventilation systems that bring fresh air into buildings, and extensive use of natural materials like wood and stone. Studies have shown that spaces incorporating biophilic design reduce stress, improve cognitive function, and enhance occupant satisfaction.

**C.** The concept of "green buildings" extends beyond aesthetic considerations to encompass comprehensive environmental performance. Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design (LEED) certification, established by the U.S. Green Building Council in 1993, has become the international standard for sustainable building practices. LEED evaluates buildings across categories including energy efficiency, water conservation, materials selection, indoor environmental quality, and innovation. Buildings achieving LEED Platinum certification, the highest rating, typically consume 25-30% less energy than conventional buildings and reduce carbon emissions by 30-35%.

**D.** Passive House standards, originating from Germany in the 1990s, represent an even more rigorous approach to building efficiency. These buildings achieve net-zero energy consumption through super-insulated envelopes, triple-glazed windows, airtight construction, and heat recovery ventilation systems. While Passive House buildings typically cost 5-10% more to construct, the additional investment is recovered within 7-10 years through reduced energy bills. The world's largest Passive House building, a residential tower in Vancouver, Canada, completed in 2017, demonstrates that these principles can be applied at scale in urban environments.

**E.** Vertical farming offers an innovative solution to urban food production, reducing the environmental costs associated with transporting food over long distances. These hydroponic or aeroponic systems grow crops in vertically stacked layers inside buildings, using 95% less water than traditional agriculture and eliminating pesticide use. Vertical farms can produce year-round harvests, independent of weather conditions, and can be located directly within cities, drastically reducing transportation emissions. However, high energy costs for lighting and climate control remain challenges that renewable energy integration must address.

**F.** The concept of the "15-minute city" popularized by Paris mayor Anne Hidalgo envisions urban design where residents can access work, school, shopping, healthcare, and leisure activities within a 15-minute walk or bicycle ride. This approach reduces car dependency, lowers carbon emissions, and promotes physical activity. Barcelona's "superblocks" initiative, which restricts car access to major roads and transforms interior streets into pedestrian-friendly public spaces, has demonstrated how existing cities can be retrofitted to support active transportation and community interaction.

**G.** Adaptive reuse of existing structures represents another sustainable strategy. Rather than demolishing and rebuilding, which generates substantial construction waste and consumes embodied energy, architects repurpose historic and industrial buildings for modern uses. The High Line in New York City, transformed from an abandoned elevated railway into a 1.45-mile public park, has become a model for urban regeneration. In Rotterdam, a 1940s grain silo was converted into residential apartments, creating unique cylindrical homes while preserving the building's iconic industrial character.

**H.** Smart city technologies integrate digital systems to optimize urban efficiency and sustainability. Internet of Things (IoT) sensors monitor air quality, traffic flow, energy consumption, and waste levels in real-time. Artificial intelligence algorithms analyze this data to optimize traffic signal timing, manage energy grids, and predict maintenance needs before failures occur. Singapore's Smart Nation initiative and Copenhagen's intelligent street lighting system, which adjusts brightness based on pedestrian presence, exemplify how technology can enhance urban sustainability while improving quality of life.

**I.** Circular economy principles are reshaping urban material flows, moving away from the "take-make-waste" linear model toward systems where waste becomes a resource. In this paradigm, building materials are designed for disassembly and reuse at end-of-life, organic waste is composted to fertilize urban gardens, and industrial byproducts become inputs for other processes. The Netherlands, which aims to become fully circular by 2050, has pioneered numerous initiatives, including Rotterdam's "Waste to Building" program that incorporates recycled construction materials into new buildings.

**J.** Social sustainability, often overlooked in favor of environmental metrics, is equally critical for truly sustainable cities. This concept encompasses affordable housing, public space accessibility, equitable access to services, and community resilience. Gentrification, which displaces long-time residents when neighborhoods become more desirable, undermines social sustainability. Successful sustainable cities must balance environmental goals with social equity, ensuring that improvements benefit all residents rather than creating exclusive, inaccessible enclaves for the wealthy.

## 📖 Reading Passage 1: The History of Tea

**Test:** Reading Practice

---

### Passage Text

**A.** The story of tea begins in China. According to legend, in 2737 BC, the Chinese emperor Shen Nung was sitting beneath a tree while his servant boiled drinking water, when some leaves from the bush above blew into the water. The emperor tasted it, found it refreshing, and tea was born. The beverage that would become the world's second most popular drink after water has been consumed in China for thousands of years.

**B.** Tea reached Europe in the early 17th century through Dutch and Portuguese traders who brought it back from the East. Initially, tea was a luxury product that only the very wealthy could afford. It was sold in coffee houses and served in special teacups and teapots made of fine porcelain, often displayed in glass cabinets to show off its value. Tea drinking became a fashionable social activity among the aristocracy and upper classes.

**C.** In 1843, the first commercial tea plantations were established in Assam, India, by the British East India Company. This marked the beginning of large-scale tea production outside China. The British developed a taste for tea that was stronger and more robust than the delicate Chinese varieties, leading to the establishment of plantations in Assam and other parts of India to meet the growing demand.

**D.** The Boston Tea Party of 1773 was a significant event in the history of tea. As a protest against British taxation without representation, American colonists dumped 342 chests of tea into Boston Harbor. This event not only escalated tensions that would lead to the American Revolution but also permanently changed American drinking habits from tea to coffee, which remains the preferred beverage in the United States today.

**E.** The tea trade had a profound impact on global economics and politics. The British Empire's control over tea production and trade routes contributed significantly to its economic power. The Opium Wars in the mid-19th century were fought in part because Britain wanted to correct the trade imbalance with China, where tea was the major import but China had little interest in British goods.

**F.** Today, tea is cultivated in more than 40 countries, with China, India, Kenya, and Sri Lanka being the largest producers. There are over 3,000 varieties of tea, but all come from the same plant species, Camellia sinensis. The differences in flavor, aroma, and appearance are due to variations in growing conditions, processing methods, and blending techniques used by tea companies.

**G.** The health benefits of tea have been celebrated for centuries. Tea contains antioxidants called polyphenols, which may help reduce the risk of heart disease and certain types of cancer. Green tea, which is minimally processed, retains the highest levels of these beneficial compounds. However, tea also contains caffeine, which can cause sleep disturbances and increased heart rate in some people, though usually in lower amounts than coffee.

**H.** Tea ceremonies have developed in various cultures, most notably in Japan with the Chanoyu ceremony. This ritualized preparation and serving of matcha, a powdered green tea, emphasizes harmony, respect, purity, and tranquility. The ceremony is not just about drinking tea but about creating a moment of mindfulness and connection with nature and others.

## 🔍 Reading Insights

**Question Type: True/False/Not Given**

**💡 Strategy:**
1. **Keyword matching:** Identify key words in the statement (names, dates, places) and scan the passage for them.
2. **Watch for absolutes:** Statements with words like "always," "only," "never," "all" are more likely to be FALSE.
3. **Not Given vs. False:** If information is completely absent, it's NOT GIVEN. If information contradicts the statement, it's FALSE.

**Example:**
- Question 5: "The Opium Wars were caused entirely by British desire..."
  - Passage says "in part" - this suggests there were other causes too
  - "Entirely" is an absolute word
  - Answer: NOT GIVEN
